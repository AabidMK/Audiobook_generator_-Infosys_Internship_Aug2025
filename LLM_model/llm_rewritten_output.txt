### The Tab Trap: A Crucial Warning!

Now, a crucial warning! Never, ever try to simulate a table using tabs, like you see in **Table 23** or **Table 27**. While it might *look* like a table on screen, it has absolutely no underlying structure for a screen reader. It's just a jumble of text and spaces. This is completely inaccessible and defeats the purpose of your data. Always use proper table elements.

### Advanced Accessibility: Setting Scope

Finally, for advanced accessibility, **Table 25** reminds us about "setting column and row scope via the tags panel." This is a more technical aspect where you explicitly tell the PDF tags which cells are headers for which data, ensuring perfect interpretation for assistive technologies.

### Wrapping Up

So, as you can see, designing accessible PDF tables involves more than just putting data into rows and columns. It's about clear headings, thoughtful structure, appropriate use of text over symbols, avoiding merged cells, and never simulating tables with tabs. By paying attention to these details, you ensure your valuable data is truly accessible and understandable to everyone who needs it.
